# Problem Aware Avatar
### Offer: A hyper-personalized, anthropomorphized AI caregiving companion (phone-first, via Telegram/WhatsApp/SMS/email) that tracks the tedious, recurring tasks of cancer caregiving — daily hygiene & care routines, platelet/blood-count tracking, medication tracking (including changes and reactions), and appointment coordination through Google Calendar.

**Audience:** Middle-income, employed adults who are caring for a loved one with cancer.
**Offer:** ~$39/month subscription that helps overwhelmed family caregivers stop dropping the balls — meds, counts, appointments, daily care — without becoming a full-time logistics manager.

---

## Stage of Awareness: PROBLEM AWARE

> *She knows exactly how much pain she's in. She feels the weight of it every single day. What she does **not** know is that a solution like this exists — or that "AI" could ever be the thing that helps her. She's not searching for a "caregiver app with an AI agent." She's lying awake Googling "how do I keep track of my mom's chemo meds" and "what does it mean when platelets are low." Her awareness lives entirely in the **problem**, not the solution.*

### A → Who Are They

- **Name:** Denise Carter
- **Gender:** Female
- **Job:** Office Manager / Billing Coordinator at a mid-size dental practice (full-time, hourly-salaried hybrid; can't easily "step away" during the day)
- **Household Income:** ~$72,000/year combined (she earns ~$52K; husband works in HVAC)
- **Marital Status:** Married, 22 years; two kids (one in college, one a junior in high school)
- **Education Level:** Associate's degree (Business Administration), some additional certifications. Practical, not academic.

Denise is 49. She is the **default caregiver** for her 74-year-old mother, who was diagnosed with non-Hodgkin lymphoma eight months ago. She has a brother who lives two states away and "helps when he can," which mostly means texting *"how's mom doing?"* on Sundays. Denise is firmly in the **sandwich generation**: a teenager at home, a parent in chemo, a job that doesn't pause, and a marriage she's trying not to neglect.

### D → Smart Market Questions

**What keeps them awake at night, eyes open, staring at the ceiling:**
*"Did I give Mom the right pill at the right time? Did I write down what the nurse said? Was that bruise on her arm normal, or is it the platelets again? What if she spikes a fever at 2 a.m. and I miss it? What if I forget the appointment on Thursday and we lose our slot?"* She replays the day's care like a checklist she's never sure she completed, and she dreads the morning because it starts the whole thing over.

**What are they secretly afraid of in life:**
That she will **make a mistake that hurts her mother** — a missed dose, an ignored symptom, a forgotten appointment — and have to live with it. Underneath that: that she isn't enough, that she's failing everyone at once, and that one day the stress will take *her* down too and there'll be no one left to catch the family.

**What are they angry about, and who are they angry at:**
Angry at how **alone** this is. Angry that her brother gets to be the "good son" with a phone call while she does the physical, emotional, and logistical labor. Angry at a healthcare system that hands her a folder of instructions and a phone number that goes to voicemail. Quietly angry at herself for being short-tempered with her kids and husband. And a deeper, unspeakable anger at the cancer itself.

**Top 3 frustrations they feel every day:**
1. **Information overload with no system** — instructions on sticky notes, in her phone, in a notebook, in three different group texts, on the fridge whiteboard. Nothing talks to anything else.
2. **The mental load never shuts off.** Even at work, she's tracking the next dose, the next refill, the next appointment in the back of her mind.
3. **Repeating herself / re-explaining** to siblings, to the on-call nurse, to herself — because there's no single source of truth for what's actually happening with Mom.

**Biggest secret desire in life:**
To feel like she has it **handled** — to be confident she's not dropping anything — so she can be *present* with her mother as a daughter again instead of a frazzled coordinator. And, quietly, to get a piece of *her own life* back.

**Built-in bias to how they make decisions:**
Risk-averse, trust-driven, and proof-driven. She decides based on whether it feels **safe, simple, and recommended by someone like her** (a nurse, a fellow caregiver, a Facebook group). She's skeptical of anything that sounds "techy," salesy, or complicated. If it takes more than a few minutes to figure out, she'll abandon it.

**Common words or language unique to them:**
"Counts." "Chemo brain." "Good days and bad days." "Her numbers." "The portal." "On-call nurse." "Caregiver burnout." "I just don't want to drop the ball." "I'm running on fumes." "One day at a time." "I don't even know what I don't know."

**Top 3 complaints about existing solutions:**
1. *"The notebook/whiteboard/phone alarms don't keep up — the second something changes, my whole system is wrong."*
2. *"The hospital app (portal) is for the doctors, not for me. It doesn't tell me what to **do**."*
3. *"Every 'app' I look at wants me to set up a million things. I don't have the time or the brainpower right now."*

### E → Going Deep

**Top 3 dominant negative emotions:**
1. **Overwhelm** — the feeling of carrying too much with no relief in sight.
2. **Fear/dread** — specifically of a mistake or a sudden crisis (a fever, a fall, a missed dose).
3. **Guilt** — that she's never doing enough, for anyone, including herself.

**Top 3 dominant positive emotions from solving this problem:**
1. **Relief** — "I can finally breathe; something's actually keeping track with me."
2. **Confidence/control** — "I know exactly where things stand, and I'll be told before something slips."
3. **Connection** — being freed up to actually *be with* her mom instead of managing her.

**Top 3 beliefs they hold about the world:**
1. If you want it done right, you usually have to do it yourself.
2. Good people take care of their family — that's just what you do.
3. Things tend to work out if you keep showing up and don't give up.

**Biggest lifestyle desire:**
A life that feels **manageable again** — where caregiving has a system she can trust, her job is secure, her family isn't running on empty, and she has even a sliver of time and peace for herself.

### F → Purchasing Habits

**Top 3 decision triggers:**
1. A **scare** — a near-miss with a dose or a missed appointment that frightens her into action.
2. A **trusted recommendation** — another caregiver in her support group or a nurse says *"this helped me."*
3. **Simplicity proof** — she can see it works in minutes, on the phone she already uses, without a complicated setup.

**Prior purchases for this pain:**
Pill organizers and dispensers, a label maker, notebooks/planners, a dry-erase fridge calendar, sticky notes in bulk, maybe a generic medication-reminder app she downloaded and abandoned. She's bought *things*, not a *system* — and none of it talks back or adapts when the plan changes.

**Price tolerance for offer:**
Cost-conscious but not cheap when it comes to her mother. She'll hesitate at anything that feels like "another subscription," but **~$39/month** lands in "less than dinner out / less than the copay" territory — payable *if* she believes it genuinely prevents mistakes and lifts the load. The framing that wins her: *"For about a dollar a day, you stop carrying all of this in your head."*

**Time horizon of solution:**
**Immediate.** She is in the storm right now. She's not researching for "someday" — she needs relief this week. If it can start helping within a day of signing up, that's the whole ballgame.

### G → Primary Wants

- **Wants to gain:** Peace of mind, a reliable system, and the confidence that nothing is slipping through the cracks.
- **Wants to be:** A *good* daughter and mother again — calm, present, and capable — instead of a frazzled coordinator.

- **Wants to save:** Time, mental energy, and her own health and sanity (and money, by avoiding crises and missed appointments).

### H → Empathy Map

**Seeing:**
A fridge covered in sticky notes and a whiteboard already out of date. A pillbox she's not 100% sure she filled correctly. Her mother dozing on the couch, paler than yesterday. Group texts from her brother she hasn't had time to answer. A patient portal full of numbers she doesn't fully understand. Other caregivers in her Facebook group saying *"I'm so burned out."*

**Thinking:**
*"Did I already give her that? What was that bruise about? When's the next blood draw? I can't forget Thursday. I'm so tired. I can't be the only one drowning like this. There has to be a better way to keep track — but I don't have time to figure one out."*

**Hearing:**
The on-call nurse's rushed instructions. Her mother saying *"don't make a fuss, I'm fine."* Her husband: *"you need to take care of yourself too."* Her brother: *"let me know if you need anything"* (and never following up). The fridge alarm she's started to tune out.

**Feeling:**
Stretched thin and bone-tired. A low hum of anxiety that never fully switches off. Guilt in every direction. A flash of resentment she immediately feels ashamed of. And, underneath it all, deep love and the terror of losing her mom.

**Saying:**
*"I'm fine, just busy." "I just don't want to drop the ball." "One day at a time." "I don't even know what I don't know." "I wish there was something that just kept track of all of it with me."*

**Doing:**
Refilling pillboxes at the kitchen table at 11 p.m. Texting siblings updates she's already exhausted from giving. Re-checking the calendar three times. Googling symptoms in the car. Apologizing to her kids for being short. Downloading apps she never finishes setting up. Quietly carrying the entire operation in her head — and bracing for the day it all becomes too much.
