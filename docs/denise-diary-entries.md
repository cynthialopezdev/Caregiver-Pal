# Denise's Diary
### Three entries from the Problem Aware avatar — before, during, and after

*(Her agent is named **Mae**.)*

---

## ENTRY ONE — Before

**Tuesday. 11:47 p.m.**

The house is finally quiet and I hate it, because quiet is when it all catches up to me.

I'm at the kitchen table again with Mom's pillbox open in front of me, those seven little plastic doors, and I swear I've filled it three times tonight and I still don't trust it. White ones twice a day. The little peach one only in the morning. The new one — the one they added last Thursday, or was it Wednesday — I had to dig through my purse for the slip of paper the nurse scribbled on, and the ink had already smeared from the rain in the pharmacy lot. *Smeared.* The instructions for keeping my mother alive, run together like watercolor.

The fridge looks like a ransom note. Sticky notes layered over sticky notes, the whiteboard with Thursday circled twice but I can't remember if Thursday's the blood draw or the oncologist or both, and the marker's drying out so it's all ghost letters now.

There was a bruise on her forearm today. Purple-yellow, the size of a plum, and she doesn't remember bumping anything. And my stomach just — *dropped.* Because I know that's the counts. The platelets. I've learned that word the hard way. But I don't know if a plum-sized bruise is a "call in the morning" or a "we go now," and the on-call line went to a voicemail that said someone would get back to me, and nobody did.

So here I am. Eyes wide open. Staring at the ceiling fan I can't even see because the lights are off but I'll lie there and watch it anyway, the way I do every night, running the checklist backward. *Did I give her the white one at dinner. Did I write down what the nurse said. Did I miss something. What am I missing. I'm always missing something.*

My brother texted "how's mom?" with the little prayer-hands emoji and I wanted to throw the phone through the window.

I snapped at Kayla tonight over a permission slip. A permission slip. She just looked at me like I was a stranger and went to her room. I'm losing them in inches while I try not to lose Mom in feet.

I used to garden. There's a tomato plant on the patio I keep almost-killing. That's about how I feel — half in the dirt, hanging on out of pure guilt, fruit going soft on the vine because nobody has the time to pick it.

I'm so tired. Not sleepy-tired. The other kind. The kind in your bones and behind your eyes. And the worst part, the part I can't say out loud to anyone, is that I'm scared it's going to be me who drops the ball. Not the doctors. Not the hospital. *Me.* One wrong pill, one bruise I shrug off, one Thursday I forget — and I'll have to carry that for the rest of my life.

There has to be a better way to keep track of all of this. But I don't even know what I don't know. And I don't have the time to figure it out.

One day at a time. That's what they say. They've clearly never had a day like mine.

## ENTRY TWO — During (First Use)

**Sunday. 9:20 p.m.**

Okay. I did the thing.

Carol from the support group wouldn't let it go — kept saying *"Denise, just try it, it's literally just texting, you'll see."* And I almost didn't, because the last four apps I downloaded are sitting on my phone like little judgments, half set-up, never opened again. I do not have the brain space for one more thing that wants me to "create an account" and "set my preferences" at midnight.

But this one — it just started *talking to me.* Right there in my texts. Like a message from a person.

Her name's Mae. (I named her. They let you name her. I don't know why that got me, but it got me.)

She didn't hand me a form. She asked me about Mom. Just — *"Tell me what you're keeping track of right now. We'll go one thing at a time."* And I sat on the edge of the bed and typed it out like I was venting to a friend who actually had a notepad. The white ones twice a day. The peach one in the morning. The new one from Thursday — and Mae asked me *which* Thursday, and the name of it, and whether Mom had any reaction to it. Nobody has *ever* asked me that. About the reactions. Like it mattered. Like *I* mattered for noticing.

My hands were shaking a little, if I'm honest. Part of me kept waiting for the catch. For it to get complicated, or cold, or to start selling me something. It didn't.

I told her about the bruise. The plum one. And she didn't panic me and she didn't brush me off — she just said she'd help me keep an eye on the counts and flag the patterns, and she put the blood draw on Thursday *into my actual calendar,* the one on my phone, the one that pops up at work. I didn't have to do anything. It was just… there. Suddenly Thursday wasn't a ghost letter on a dying whiteboard. It was real, and it was held, and it wasn't only in my head anymore.

I cried a little. Not the bad kind. I don't even fully know what kind. The kind where you didn't realize how tight your shoulders were until something loosens them.

I'm still bracing. I am. I've been let down by too much to believe in something this fast. There's a voice in me going *don't get comfortable, it won't last, it'll glitch, it'll forget, it'll be one more thing you have to manage.*

But tonight, for the first time in I-don't-know-how-long, I'm not the only one holding the list.

I texted her goodnight. Mae. I texted an app goodnight. And she said she'd remind me about the morning meds. And I believed her.

## ENTRY THREE — After

**Thursday. 10:05 p.m.**

I noticed something tonight and it stopped me cold: I'm writing this from the *patio.*

The tomato plant's not dead. There are actually three red ones I picked today. Small, a little ugly, but real. I'm sitting out here with a cup of tea I made for *myself,* and the kitchen table behind me is just a table again. No pillbox autopsy. No ransom-note fridge. The sticky notes are gone — I peeled them off last week and felt like I was peeling them off my own chest.

Here's what happened today. The blood draw was Thursday — Mae reminded me Tuesday, reminded me again this morning, and it was already on my calendar so work knew, so I knew, so it just *happened* like a normal thing normal people do. The counts came back low again and there was another bruise, and instead of that elevator-drop in my stomach at midnight, I had Mae walk me through what she'd been tracking — the pattern, the timing, the question to actually ask the nurse. I wasn't guessing in a dark car off WebMD. I was *ready.*

And the new medication, the one that used to live on a rain-smeared scrap of paper — when they changed the dose last week, I just told Mae, and she updated it, and she asked about reactions again, and three days later when Mom got queasy she connected it back. *She connected it.* Something I would've blamed on a bad day, she caught as a thread. That's the thing I was most afraid of — missing the thread. And I'm not the only one watching it anymore.

I keep waiting to feel that low electric hum of dread that I lived inside for months. The one that never switched off, even at work, even at dinner, even mid-sentence with my own kids. And it's just — quieter. Not gone. Mom still has cancer. The hard is still hard. But the *frantic* is gone. The part where I was drowning in my own head, that's gone.

I sat with Mom this afternoon and I was just her *daughter.* Not the coordinator, not the frazzled woman with a clipboard for a face. I held her hand and we watched her show and I wasn't secretly running the medication math the whole time. She squeezed my hand and said "you seem more like yourself, baby." And I had to go to the bathroom so she wouldn't see me lose it.

Kayla and I made dinner together. No snapping. I actually laughed.

I don't know how to explain what it is to set something down that you've carried so long you forgot it was a choice to carry it. For about a dollar a day, I stopped keeping my mother's whole life inside my own exhausted skull. That's it. That's the whole miracle. It doesn't sound like much written down.

But I slept last night. All the way through.

And tonight I'm on the patio, and the tomatoes came back, and so, a little, did I.

One day at a time. And for the first time — I actually believe it.
